# Layoffs and Volatility

> *The only constant in corporate life is that someone, somewhere, is preparing a slide deck about "right-sizing."*

Welcome to 2026, where layoffs are no longer a temporary crisis—they are a permanent feature of the corporate hellscape. The job market is absolutely flooded. Every other week, a tech giant announces they are laying off thousands of employees while simultaneously posting record profits. If you are lucky enough to still have a job, you quickly arrive at a sobering realization: the traditional career playbook is broken, and loyalty has officially been priced out of the market.

People love to invent god-awful terms for this. They call it "quiet quitting," "job hugging," or "coasting." But let's be clear: realizing that your company views you as a line-item on a spreadsheet and acting accordingly is not a trend. It's called being a normal human being, not a corporate robot. 

## The Economics of Staying Put

In this environment, you have to be tactical about mobility. Quitting your job without having another one lined up is basically financial suicide. The days of taking six months off to "find yourself" and casually walking into a Senior PM role are over. 

But even leaving for another job is wildly risky. Let's say you manage to jump ship to a competitor. Congratulations: you are now the new guy. In the corporate execution block, the new guy is always the easiest head to chop when the next round of layoffs comes around. You have no institutional knowledge, no protected relationships, and your severance package is laughably cheap. You are the structural equivalent of the red shirt on *Star Trek*. 

So basically, you are staying put. You are hunkering down in your current role and riding out the storm. But staying is no easy task. When you stay, you have to watch everyone around you get laid off, quit, or slowly lose their minds.

## The Infrastructure of Fear

Modern organizations are no longer based on vision, culture, or even profit—they run on pure, unfiltered fear. Everyone thinks that if they just work a little bit harder, if they just stay online a little bit later, they might be safe. They believe that producing more output will create an invisible shield around their livelihood. 

This is a delusion.

In reality, there is basically nothing you can do to guarantee your safety. You cannot outwork a macro-economic pivot orchestrated by a consulting firm that doesn't know your name. The only rational response to this scenario is "good enough." If the reward for 100% effort is the exact same underlying anxiety as 50% effort, then delivering anything above the threshold of "not fired" is simply bad accounting on your part. 

And people are right to be scared and confused. The company will actively foster this disorientation, usually through the vehicle of the perpetual reorg.

## The Restructuring Carousel

If your company isn't laying people off, they are almost certainly reorganizing. They will fire a chunk of the staff and then shuffle around the remaining half. The stated purpose of this is "strategic alignment." The actual purpose is to keep employees entirely disoriented and firmly in their place. 

Once you start to get your bearings—once you understand who reports to whom, what your actual job is, and how to do it efficiently—there will immediately be another reorg. Often, this happens before the previous reorg was even fully explained, just to keep you guessing. 

During these phases of transition, the company will operate under the vaguest possible directives. Leadership will stand on an All-Hands stage and confidently instruct the company to: "Make more money. Do not spend money unless it helps you print a lot *more* money." 

Then, human resources will step in. They will mandate meetings and workshops about "improving your elevator pitch" or "touching up your professional presence on LinkedIn." They will pitch this as an investment in your personal branding. What it actually is, is leadership giving you a massive wink and saying, "Hey, you are probably going to need this for your next job interview very soon." It’s pre-severance training.

## The AI Scapegoat

AI has accelerated the justification for all this headcount reduction, but mostly just functionally. It serves as the ultimate scapegoat. 

The reality is that companies overhired for years. Especially in big tech, they hoarded talent like Pokémon cards, betting that the talent would eventually blow up into something profitable. It didn't. And now, they finally have a convenient, investor-friendly excuse to purge the bloat: "We're using AI, and it's replacing everyone."

No, it's really not. 

Don't get me wrong, employees are using the shit out of AI. We are leaning on it heavily just to survive the increased workload. But it is not replacing the white-collar knowledge worker. It’s simply giving executives a futuristic-sounding explanation for why they fired the guy who spent the last three years running A/B tests on the color of a "Submit" button.

## The Tactical Advantage of the Departed

There is a silver lining to the modern reality of constant layoffs, restructuring, and high turnover: the sheer volume of people who are no longer in the building. A steady stream of exiting employees is an absolute gift to your own survival, because it provides you with the ultimate corporate get-out-of-jail-free card. 

The strategy is simple: whenever things break, issues arise, or a massive systemic failure lands on your desk, you blame it entirely on decisions made by people who no longer work there.

Now, you can't just throw your former colleagues under the bus explicitly. You can't say, "Kevin was an idiot and hardcoded the staging environment to point to production before he got laid off in Q3." That makes you look petty, and corporate doesn't like petty—unless it's formalized into a performance review. Instead, you let AI translate your very specific finger-pointing into a well-defended, perfectly sanitized "improvement process." 

You tell the AI, "Write an incident report explaining that Kevin broke this before he left, and I had to fix it." The AI obediently strips away the malice and returns a majestic piece of corporate prose: *"A root cause analysis has identified legacy architectural decisions and undocumented, suboptimal processes from prior development cycles as the primary catalyst for the recent instability."* 

Beautiful. You didn't blame Kevin; you blamed *legacy architectural decisions*. Kevin is gone, he's hopefully moved on to a job where he's tormenting someone else's codebase, and frankly, he doesn't give a shit what you blame him for. No one is calling Kevin to check his alibi.

From there, you ride the AI-generated momentum all the way to the bank. You take the AI output and propose a "reformed framework" that will be rigorously analyzed and implemented to ensure that—and this is the key phrase you must always include in the slide deck—*"this never happens again."* 

Spoiler alert: it absolutely will happen again. But by the time it does, you'll either have a new AI-generated process to blame, or better yet, you'll be the one who left, giving the next poor bastard the opportunity to perfectly execute this strategy against you.

## The Vulture Trap

Let's talk about the immediate aftermath of a departure. When someone on your team abruptly leaves—whether they quit or were escorted out of the Slack workspace—a very specific and pathetic corporate phenomenon occurs. Your remaining colleagues will descend on the departed's abandoned projects like rabid vultures to a fresh kill. 

They do this because they are desperate to be seen as "valuable" in an ecosystem where value is an imaginary construct. They think that volunteering for an orphaned Jira ticket is a display of initiative that leadership will miraculously reward.

Do not, under any circumstances, participate in this feeding frenzy.

If you voluntarily reach out and say, "I can take over Sarah's Q3 deliverables," you are not being a team player. You are being an idiot. You are actively volunteering to be sandbagged with someone else's discarded, half-finished, undocumented garbage. You are picking up a project that was abruptly interrupted, meaning you will spend the first three weeks just trying to figure out where Sarah hid the staging database credentials before she rage-quit. 

The corporate myth insists that if you "step up" in a time of crisis, leadership will recognize your heroism and reward you accordingly. This is laughable. If you are extremely lucky, you might get a fleeting verbal shout-out at the end of a sprint review that no one is paying attention to anyway. That is the ceiling of your compensation. Volunteering for someone else's work will never translate to more money, a better title, or increased job security. It just translates to more work.

Let the vultures have the carcass. Do not add a single task to your plate unless you are explicitly instructed to do so by your manager. And even then, make sure it is recognized as a formal reallocation of your responsibilities, not a fun little side quest. Your job is to do exactly what your manager asks you to do, and absolutely nothing more. Let someone else prowl the graveyard for extra weight to carry.

## Optimizing the Emptiness

Because half your colleagues no longer exist, you are primed to pull off one of the greatest tricks in corporate survival: removing almost all meetings from your calendar. 

If there are no teams or leaders left, you will be shocked by how quickly the entire operational structure breaks down. You can use this vacuum to your advantage. If you have a daily standup, propose that the team move to weekly standups to "optimize time with fewer team resources." If you have recurring check-ins or analytics readouts, strongly suggest moving all of these to an asynchronous format. 

Even better, use AI agents to fully automate the delivery of these dashboards and insights. Yes, these AI-generated insights will be completely ignored—but the regular ones were being ignored anyway. The only difference is that now there will be a significantly higher volume of them, and management will have to sift through ten genuinely bad, hallucinated insights to find one that is marginally okay. 

Your overarching narrative here is: "Hey, we're using AI to do more with less." 

In reality, you are just doing less. 

## Your Next Move

Job security is a complete myth. This has been written about enough that I won't rehash all of it here, but suffice to say: this is not your boomer parents' corporate world. No one gets a gold watch after forty years of service anymore. 

With this reality in mind, you need to be strategic about the free time you are slowly clawing back. Once you've successfully automated your remaining responsibilities and shifted the burden of accountability onto the ghosts of your former coworkers, you will start to accumulate actual, usable hours in your workday. 

Do not waste all of this time scrolling through TikTok or arguing on X. 

Use this time to figure out your next move. It doesn't have to be anything grandiose—you don't need to launch a VC-backed startup from your kitchen table. Just learn something. Read a book. Teach yourself a new framework or play around with a different AI model. Always be improving yourself, because you will inevitably have a next job. 

It is a statistical certainty that you will not retire at your current company. Some people reading this will literally never be able to retire at all. The company is actively preparing to replace you the second it becomes economically viable. The least you can do is use their time and their AI subscriptions to make sure you're ready when they finally figure out how to do it.
