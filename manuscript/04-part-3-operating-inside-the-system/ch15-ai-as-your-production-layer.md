## Acquiring the Tools (On the Company's Dime)

Before you can build an AI production layer, you need the actual tools. For most knowledge work, the baseline recommendation is straightforward: you want access to OpenAI Enterprise, ChatGPT Team, or the Anthropic Claude equivalent. These are the current envelope-pushers, giving you access to the best models at near-unlimited capacity. 

Getting access to these tools is your first order of business, but there is one absolute, non-negotiable rule: **You should never pay for these tools yourself.**

If your company expects you to operate in the modern ecosystem, the company pays the $20-a-month subscription fee. Period. But because corporate procurement is a nightmare designed by accountants who hate joy, you usually can't just slap an AI subscription on the corporate Amex without raising an eyebrow. 

You have to justify it. And obviously, the best way to justify getting an AI tool is to have an AI tool write the justification.

Open up whatever free, basic AI version you currently have access to—or just use your phone—and ask it to draft a well-defended, properly documented business case addressed to your manager explaining exactly why you need the enterprise version to "optimize your deliverables." The key is to lean heavily into two concepts that make corporate IT departments sweat: *legacy* and *security*. 

Have the AI explain that relying on the free version limits you to "legacy infrastructure and dated models." Then, have it emphasize that the lack of proper enterprise guardrails in the free tiers creates "unacceptable data security vulnerabilities for proprietary internal IP." No manager wants to be the guy who officially forced you to keep pasting sensitive code into a public model just to save twenty bucks a month. They will almost certainly approve the expense.

Once you have access, there's a pleasant side effect to this corporate expense. Because these enterprise subscriptions are essentially unlimited—they just rate-limit you rather than cutting you off entirely—you absolutely should take them home with you. Use the company's subscription to power your personal projects, automate your personal life, and figure out your next career move. You are extracting maximum value from an organization that views you as expendable. Treat their AI allocation the exact same way.

### The Free Tier Survival Plan

If your company is experiencing a budget freeze so severe that they won't even shell out twenty bucks, that's fine. You absolutely must still use AI to survive. You'll just have to scramble a bit harder.

You'll need to string together a workflow utilizing several free versions. Google's Gemini will likely be your primary workhorse for this, mostly because Google's infinite money glitch from search ads allows them to offer a shockingly generous free tier. 

Operating on the free layer requires slightly more discipline. You have to be genuinely careful not to paste absolutely private, highly sensitive company data into a public prompt box. You are technically violating IT policies by doing so, and you will eventually get caught if you're sloppy. Anonymize the data, strip out the client names, genericize the financial figures, and *then* run it through the machine. 

This isn't a technical manual, and there are already thousands of extremely boring, SEO-optimized guides online about how to configure specific tool stacks. We won't go into agonizing detail on the exact setups here. If you have questions about which dashboard button to click, just type it into the AI, stupid.
