# Good Enough Is the New Great

> *Perfection used to be the enemy of good. Now "good enough" killed them both.*

## The Era of Identifiable Failure

If you’ve been working in white-collar jobs for more than five minutes, you probably remember the dark ages—or maybe they were the light ages, depending on your perspective—before generative AI became the mandatory corporate operating system. Back then, when someone did a bad job, it was delightfully obvious. 

In the olden times, incorrect work usually manifested as *incomplete* work. If you asked a coworker for a competitive analysis and they hadn’t done the reading, you didn't get a beautifully formatted ten-page document full of confident hallucinations. You got half a slide deck with "TBD" written in bullet points. If someone didn't know the answer to a question in a quarterly review, they stammered, they hemmed, they dodged. If a project plan was garbage, it was visibly garbage. It had missing sections, formatting that made your eyes bleed, and paragraphs that trailed off into nothing. You could spot bad work from across the room because creating completely synthesized, perfectly structured bullshit actually required almost as much effort as just doing the real work.

We had a built-in mechanism for spotting incompetence, and it was sheer visual incompleteness. The effort required to fake completeness was too high. 

Then AI entered the chat.

AI doesn't do incomplete. By its very nature, it defaults to formatting, structure, and confident delivery. It will give you an introductory paragraph, three bullet points of synthesized garbage, and a conclusion summarizing the garbage it just delivered. It wraps everything in the aesthetic of competence. 

And here’s the terrifying psychological reality of the modern corporate worker: if a document is complete, it *looks* correct. If it has bolded headers, a logical flow, and spans three formatted pages, the monkey brain of your manager or stakeholder looks at it and thinks, "Wow, somebody worked hard on this. This must be right." Nobody actually reads the words. We are an aesthetic-driven species. We evaluate the *shape* of the work, not the substance. 

So now we’ve arrived at a bizarre inflection point where anyone, at any level, can produce an absolute mountain of shit at the push of a button. And because it looks like a thoroughly researched, well-formatted mountain, the standard assessment has shifted. "Good enough" is the new great. Actually, scratch that. "Complete" is the new perfect. It’s so good-looking that nobody ever bothers to look at it closely, which means we can all just rubber-stamp it and mark the task as done.

## The Two-Minute Miracle

Let’s be honest about what’s actually happening in your home office right now. 

You’re tasked with something that, five years ago, would have been a "week-long project." Let's say it's synthesizing customer feedback interviews into a Q3 product roadmap strategy document. Five years ago, you'd have to listen to the calls, take notes, pull out themes, agonize over how to categorize them, struggle to write corporate-friendly summaries, build a narrative, and spend three days adjusting the margins in a Google Doc. You would have had syncs. Touchpoints. Status updates. You would have complained to your spouse about how hard you were working.

Today? You take the raw transcripts, dump them into whatever LLM you prefer (or whatever one your company hasn't blocked yet), and write a prompt: "Turn these transcripts into a Q3 product roadmap strategy document. Use confident corporate language. Break it into three core themes with actionable next steps."

Enter. Wait fifteen seconds. 

You copy the output. You paste it into the official company template. You spend maybe two minutes reading the bold headers to make sure it didn't accidentally declare war on a sovereign nation or invent a product feature out of thin air (though honestly, if it did, marketing would probably just run with it). You adjust a font. You save. You send it.

A week-long project turned into a two-minute copy-paste session. 

And the wildest part? Everyone pats themselves on the back. They reply in the Slack thread: "Incredible work on this, Myles. Very thorough." "Great synthesis, team!" "Aligns perfectly with our OKRs."

You didn't synthesize shit. You acted as a glorified data-ferry, hauling text from a transcript to a black box and then dumping the output into a template. You spent five minutes on it. And look, let's be fair to the machines here—the AI is actually really good now. Most of the time, this shit really *is* fine. Assuming what you are working on does not fundamentally matter (which, spoiler alert: it doesn't), the AI's output is practically indistinguishable from the output of a panicked mid-level manager working until midnight on a Tuesday. 

It is a massive time-saver. It is a genuine miracle of technology. You have successfully hacked the matrix of corporate productivity, generating the exact artifact required to advance your piece on the board without actually expending any cognitive calories. Ideally, what you make is so dense, so conclusive, and so seemingly complete that you don't even have to have a meeting to discuss it. The artifact simply exists to say, "The work has been done," and its results sound good enough for someone higher up the chain to rubber-stamp it and move on to their next existential crisis. 

This is the system functioning as designed in the modern era.

## The Mandatory Artifact

At this point, you might be asking a very reasonable question: *Why are we even writing this document?* If it has absolutely no business value, and I'm just copy-pasting it from an LLM, and my manager is just rubber-stamping it without reading it—what is the point?

The point is that the artifact is mandatory. 

Nobody wakes up and decides to write a Q3 product roadmap strategy document for fun. You are writing it because someone required it. And they required it for one of three reasons:
1. Somebody up the chain needed to look busy giving you a directive.
2. Something bad happened three years ago, so now "everything must be documented" to ensure it never happens again (it will happen again).
3. We need to say we have a process so that if things go sideways, we can point to the document and claim we followed the process.

Producing the artifact is non-negotiable. It proves you exist. It proves the manager managing you exists. 

In the past, the smart play was often to just... not do it. You would quietly ignore the request. About 80% of the time, nobody followed up, and the requirement vanished into the corporate ether. On the rare occasion you did get caught, you would just write it on the fly, frantically typing out a bare-bones summary while on the Zoom call where they finally asked for it. 

But now? Now the cost of generating the artifact is literally two minutes. It is easier to just spawn the 10-page document into existence using AI than it is to risk the 20% chance of getting caught not doing it. The friction of compliance has dropped to zero. So you produce it, the machine consumes it, and the wheel keeps turning.

## The Hollow Heart of the Output Machine

But let's acknowledge the elephant in the home office. Sometimes, you sit there, looking at this completely automated output, and you feel… weird. Empty. Hollow. 

You think: *Is this it? Is my entire professional value just stringing together prompts? Some of this document is probably total BS. Some of it might be slightly wrong. I didn't even read the third paragraph. I am a fraud.*

If you care about reality, if you care about craftsmanship, if you seek fulfillment from your labor, this whole process feels incredibly cheap. It feels like you're cheating, and worse, it feels like nobody cares that you're cheating because everyone else is silently cheating too.

You are not wrong for feeling this way. It is a deeply weird way to spend the majority of your waking hours on earth. 

But here is my advice as a cynic and a survivor: **you need to shelve it.** Take that desire for deep professional fulfillment, put it in a Tupperware container, snap the lid tight, and put it in the back of the fridge. Reallocate it. If you want to build something real, if you want to experience the deep satisfaction of craftsmanship, take up woodworking. Learn to rebuild motorcycles. Bake artisanal bread. Write a novel. Raise a well-adjusted dog.

This corporate environment is not the place for fulfillment. It is the place for survival, and survival means providing the machine with the inputs it requires so that it continues to dispense the currency you need to live your actual life. If the machine accepts AI-generated slop wrapped in a nice template, then by God, you feed it the slop. 

Do not disrupt the game you are playing. If you have to make something, or generate something, or God forbid "do" anything, you use the tool that reduces your effort to near zero. Instead of feeling guilty that you turned a forty-hour project into a two-minute copy-paste session, you need to feel *triumphant*. You have reclaimed thirty-nine hours and fifty-eight minutes of your life. That is not a hollow victory. That is a heist. You should be wearing a tuxedo and celebrating in a vault.

Sure, sometimes what you make is BS. Sometimes it is slightly wrong. Sometimes it misses nuances that a human might have caught after three days of agonizing analysis. But *literally nobody gives a shit*. And that is not necessarily a bad thing! It’s incredibly freeing once you accept it. If nobody gives a shit, you don't have to carry the mental burden of perfection. You don't have to lose sleep over the third bullet point on slide twelve. It’s just part of the game. Let the AI write it, let the manager rubber-stamp it, and go eat a sandwich in your kitchen in peace.

## Why Nobody Gives a Shit (The Mechanics of Apathy)

So why is this happening? Why has the entire corporate apparatus collectively agreed to accept "good enough" AI generation without looking too closely at the details? Why does nobody scrutinize the work anymore?

There are two massive structural reasons for this apathy.

### 1. Information Overload and the Slop Deluge

People were already overloaded with information in the digital age. Before AI, we were already drowning in a sea of Slack notifications, Jira notifications, automated reports, endless email chains, and shared Google Docs. The human brain was not designed to process the sheer volume of text that a modern knowledge worker is subjected to on a daily basis. We were already skimming. We were already at our breaking point.

And then AI came along and poured gasoline on the fire. 

Now, it’s not just human-generated information. It is AI-generated slop layered on top of the real stuff. Every single person in your organization suddenly has the ability to produce a 5,000-word analysis document in three minutes. And they are doing it, because producing that document proves they are "adding value." So now, your poor manager isn't just reviewing one weekly brief; they are getting peppered with incredibly dense, perfectly formatted, AI-generated novellas from every direct report they have. 

When you are drowning in slop, you do not pull out a magnifying glass to inspect the slop. You just try to keep your head above it. The only rational survival strategy for anyone receiving this much information is to aggressively skim, look for the bolded headers, verify that the artifact *exists*, and move on. Scrutiny requires time. Time requires bandwidth. Bandwidth has been completely annihilated by the very tool that was supposed to save us all time. It is a beautiful, self-defeating loop. Thus: the rubber stamp.

### 2. The Perpetual Fear of Layoffs

The second reason nobody looks too closely is that the modern corporate environment is largely operating in a state of perpetual fear. 

Layoffs, reorgs, downsizing, "right-sizing," "shifting priorities to align with macroeconomic headwinds"—whatever sanitized HR jargon your company is using this week, the reality is that job security is a myth. Everyone is hyper-aware that they could be cut at any moment to make the quarterly spreadsheet look better for a remote group of shareholders.

In that environment, what matters most? Visibility. The perception of productivity. 

If your coworker generates a massive, AI-assisted project plan, are you going to raise your hand and say, "Actually, I read this deeply, and paragraph four doesn't make much sense, and I think this is AI-generated noise"? No. Of course you aren't. Because poking holes in someone else's performative output just draws attention to your own performative output. Mutually Assured Destruction. If you start demanding high-quality, human-crafted perfection from them, they will demand it from you. 

Furthermore, managers who are terrified of being laid off themselves need their teams to look wildly productive so that they, as managers, look indispensable. A manager whose team produces exactly what was asked for, on time, and completely formatted, looks like a great manager. So they have zero incentive to look under the hood. If the engine sounds fine and the car is moving forward, they are absolutely not going to stop the car to check if the pistons are actually made of code-generated papier-mâché. 

The system survives on the illusion of productivity. AI provides the ultimate fuel for that illusion. 

## The Economics of Diminishing Returns

There is a hard economic reality behind this unspoken truce, and if you have any background in business, it should be glaringly obvious: the law of diminishing returns on quality.

In the physical world, quality scales linearly with cost and time up to a certain point, then exponentially increases in cost as you approach perfection. If you are building a bridge, you pay whatever it costs for that last twenty percent of engineering perfection because the alternative is the bridge collapsing into a river. Perfection is non-negotiable. If you are a surgeon, you do not stop at "good enough" when reattaching a retina. 

But in corporate knowledge work? In the land of Q3 align-o-thons and holistic growth synergies? Quality beyond baseline adequacy is utterly worthless. 

Let's look at the math. If an AI can generate a document that is 80% correct, grammatically flawless, and perfectly formatted in three minutes, the cost of that document is roughly three minutes of an employee's salary plus fraction-of-a-cent API fees. 

To bridge the gap from 80% correct to 100% correct—to hunt down the nuanced historical context of a client, to verify the exact quarter a specific initiative failed three years ago, to adjust the prose from "AI-confident" to "humanly persuasive"—takes actual work. It might take three hours. It might take a day. 

Is the business value of that document increased by the equivalent of a full day of salary because it went from 80% accurate to 100% accurate? 

Absolutely not. The business value of the document is zero in both scenarios. Its only function is to exist so a box can be checked on an OKR tracker. The ROI on perfection in middle management is aggressively negative. Every minute you spend improving an artifact past the point of "competent enough to be rubber-stamped" is a minute of your life you are effectively donating to a corporation that would lay you off on a Tuesday afternoon if their stock dipped three percent.

## The Unspoken Truce

We have reached An Unspoken Corporate Truce. 

You agree to use AI to generate the artifacts the company demands at lightning speed, creating the illusion of a highly effective, incredibly fast worker. The company, via its exhausted, terrified, slop-drowning middle managers, agrees to accept these artifacts at face value because they prove that processes are being followed and work is being "done." 

Nobody asks too many questions. Nobody demands to see the rough drafts. Nobody looks closely at the margins. As long as the document doesn't explicitly recommend committing felonies on the company dime, it is "good enough." And good enough is all you need to survive.

Is it absurd? Absolutely. Does it feel mildly insane that a terrifying percentage of the modern economy is just large language models confidently bullshitting other language models while humans occasionally click "Approve" like bored safety operators on an automated assembly line? Yes. 

But remember the thesis: you are not here to fix the system. You are not the hero in an HBO drama about corporate reform. You are a biological organism trying to secure resources in a deeply weird landscape. If the system wants volume over accuracy, shape over substance, and "good enough" over perfection, then you adapt. You lower your standards. You let go of the lingering pride tying your self-worth to a PowerPoint deck. You embrace the slop. 

And then you close your laptop at 3:00 PM and go live your actual life.
