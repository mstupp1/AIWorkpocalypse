# The Great Normalization

> _The machines didn't replace us. They just made everyone's output look the same._

## Holy Shit, Have You Seen This Thing?

When ChatGPT dropped in late 2022, I had one of those moments where the entire trajectory of your professional life recalibrates in real time. I remember typing in some dumb question — I don't even remember what — and watching the response come back and thinking: _holy shit. This is the craziest thing I've ever seen._

I'm not an oracle. I don't claim to have predicted the future. But I'm plugged in enough to recognize that a black box that produces semi-accurate text on demand might, possibly, conceivably disrupt a couple of things. Like, say, the entire concept of knowledge work. Call it intuition. Call it being chronically online. Call it whatever you want. The point is, some people saw this thing and immediately understood the implications, and some people looked at it and went "huh, neat" and went back to manually formatting their PowerPoint slides. I will never understand the second group.

So naturally, I started using it at work almost immediately.

At first, I thought I had to keep it a secret. And honestly? In those early days, the secrecy felt justified. People were rightfully skeptical. Data privacy is a real thing. Companies were issuing vague statements about "not putting proprietary information into external AI tools," which is corporate for "we haven't figured out what our policy is yet, so please don't make this our problem." But here's the thing — it's pretty easy to keep your prompts general. You don't need to paste in customer data to get AI to write you a solid first draft of a project update. You just need to tell it what kind of thing you're writing, give it some structure, and let it go.

And more importantly: if the company _really_ wanted to block it, they could have. They could've put it on the restricted URL list. They could've added it to the security blacklist. They could've sent one of those passive-aggressive IT emails that says "please refrain from using unauthorized tools" with a link to the approved software list that hasn't been updated since 2019. But they didn't. And in my book — literally this book — if the company doesn't block something, that's an endorsement. They want you to maximize revenue? Cool. Using magic might help.

And I say "magic" deliberately, because if work is a game — and it _is_ a game; if you haven't accepted this yet, you will by the end of this book — then magic is the best class to play. I'm a video game nerd, among other things, and in every RPG I've ever played, if magic is available, you use it. You don't voluntarily handicap yourself by choosing the melee class when you have access to spells that can one-shot entire encounters. That would be stupid. Using AI at work before it was officially sanctioned was like finding a spell tome in the first dungeon that the developers forgot to level-gate. Of course you're going to equip it.

## From Secret Weapon to Corporate Mandate

The secrecy phase lasted about five minutes, historically speaking.

By the time the models evolved — GPT-4 in early 2023, Claude 3.5 rolling out in mid-2024 — the outputs got noticeably better, the corporate hesitation evaporated, and suddenly every company on earth was scrambling to figure out their "AI strategy." It went from "don't tell anyone you're using this" to "everyone needs to be using this or we're fucked" in what felt like eighteen months. Maybe less.

The pivot was whiplash-inducing. One quarter, your company is sending cautious emails about data governance and responsible AI usage. The next quarter, the CEO is standing on a stage at the all-hands saying things like "we need to be an AI-first organization" and "if you're not leveraging AI in your daily workflow, you're falling behind." Falling behind _what_, exactly? Behind the competitors who are also pretending to use AI strategically while mostly using it to write slightly better emails? Sure. Let's race to that finish line.

But the mandate was real, even if the strategy behind it was vapor. And once the mandate landed, something fundamental changed about how work got done.

## The Floodgates of Slop

Here's what happened when AI went from a secret individual tool to a sanctioned corporate one: output volume went through the roof, and output quality went to the floor.

Think about how work used to operate. Before AI, if someone had to write a strategy document, they had to actually _think about it_. They had to sit down, consider the problem, organize their thoughts, type sentences that reflected those thoughts, review those sentences, probably hate those sentences, rewrite them, and eventually produce something that was at least coherent enough to circulate. The friction was the quality control. The effort of writing was also the effort of thinking.

Now? You paste some context, describe what you need in two sentences, and get a wall of semi-defensible text. Is it good? Sure, technically. Is it thoughtful? Absolutely not. Does it matter? Here's the kicker — _almost never_.

Because in today's corporate environment, nobody is going to make you defend that document unless it's actually important. And it's almost never actually important. The strategy doc goes into Confluence, gets linked in a Slack thread, gets skimmed by two people, and serves its primary purpose: existing. It exists. It was produced. The task is complete. The Jira ticket is closed. The artifact has been generated. Mission accomplished.

And if someone _does_ care enough to scrutinize it? They're going to dump your AI-generated text right back into an AI to get a summary, because they don't have time to read the whole thing either. It's AI all the way down — a decoder ring translating encoded bullshit back into decoded bullshit, with each pass burning a little more compute power and a little more of the environment. But nobody's tracking that, because we're too busy tracking sprint velocity.

Dictionary.com named "slop" the word of the year in 2024, and frankly, the timing was perfect. The entire internet — and by extension, the entire corporate knowledge base — was rapidly filling with AI-generated content that was technically fine but spiritually empty. Slop. Not wrong, but not right either. Not insightful, but not offensive. Just... there. A massive, growing blob of adequately-worded noise.

## The Death of Visible Bad Work

Here's the thing that nobody talks about, and it's maybe the most important shift AI created in the workplace: bad work used to be _visible_.

Before AI, if someone didn't do their work, you could tell. The document didn't exist. The analysis wasn't done. The proposal was half a page with obvious gaps. Bad work was identifiable because it took almost as much effort to produce bad work as it did to produce good work. Nobody was going to write a five-page essay on something they hadn't thought through — the act of writing that essay would force some amount of thinking, or the gaps would be glaringly obvious to anyone reading it.

AI killed that signal.

Now, someone who has put zero thought into a problem can generate a comprehensive, well-structured, properly formatted document about it in about ninety seconds. It'll have headers. It'll have bullet points. It'll reference relevant considerations. It'll even have a conclusion with recommended next steps. The only thing it won't have is any actual thinking behind it. But good luck spotting that from the outside, because the output _looks_ exactly like something a competent person would produce after a week of analysis.

This is the normalization. Not that AI made everyone's work great — it didn't. It made everyone's work look the same. The floor rose, and the ceiling blurred, and now managers are sitting in a sea of uniformly adequate output with absolutely no idea who's actually good at their job and who's just good at prompting.

## The Integration of Everything

AI didn't just become a tool people use. It became the infrastructure.

Employees are using multiple AI platforms — ChatGPT for some things, Claude for others, Gemini because their company has a Google Workspace deal and it's just _there_, Copilot because Microsoft jammed it into every product they make whether you asked for it or not. People are generating code with AI, writing all their replies with AI, producing analysis with AI. The human is technically "in the loop," but let's be honest about what that loop looks like: you're a garbage dump operator transferring slop from one pile to another. You take an AI-generated email, paste it into an AI-powered project management tool, which generates AI-suggested requirements, which get reviewed by a human who uses AI to write their feedback. At some point the word "human" in "human in the loop" becomes more of a technicality than a description.

It's not just employees, either. AI got integrated into the _business itself_. Every company had to become an "AI company" — not because it made sense, not because they had a clear use case, but because if you're not an AI company, you're not innovative, and if you're not innovative, China will fuck us. Or something. Yesterday the big corporate buzzword was "digital transformation." Before that it was "big data." Before that it was literally just having a website. Now it's "AI for Everything" — which is a real initiative name I've seen in the wild, and I want you to appreciate that someone wrote that on a slide, presented it to executives, and nobody said, "Hey, maybe AI where it's useful or helpful? Does it have to be Everything with a capital E?"

It does, apparently. Capital E. Everything.

And it's integrated into the software itself — the tools you use every day, the products your company makes or sells. Every SaaS product now has an AI sidebar. Every developer tool has an AI copilot. Every analytics platform has an AI-powered insights feature. Whether any of this is actually useful is a separate question that nobody in a position of authority seems interested in answering.

## The Financial Circus

There's a whole parallel story happening on the financial side of all this, and while it's not the main focus of this book, it's worth understanding because it directly affects how and why AI got shoved into every corner of your professional life.

The classic Silicon Valley playbook played out in real time: build the thing, give it away for free, acquire users, figure out the money later. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — they all gave away an absurd amount of AI capability for either free or dirt cheap. Google effectively has the infinite money code — they print cash from search ads and YouTube, so they can afford to subsidize AI products basically forever and not blink. The others raised billions from investors who were terrified of being left behind and wrote checks with a level of enthusiasm typically reserved for cocaine purchases at music festivals.

And for a while, it was a golden age for consumers. You could use these incredibly powerful tools for free. Even the subscription tiers were basically losing money because people were absolutely _abusing_ them — running hundreds of complex queries a day, generating entire codebases, having the thing write their novel for them — and the companies were eating the compute costs because user growth mattered more than unit economics. It was glorious. A brief, beautiful window where the most powerful technology ever created was essentially free to use without limits.

Then the bills came due. Ads started appearing. API pricing went through the roof. Usage caps got tightened. The "unlimited" plan quietly became the "we'll throttle you after 50 messages" plan. The classic bait-and-switch, except this time the bait was _the fundamental reorganization of how human beings produce work_ and the switch was $200/month plus metered usage. Standard stuff.

## The New Normal

So here we are. AI is normal now. It's as normal as email. As normal as Slack. As normal as the performative busyness that defines corporate culture. It's baked into everything — your tools, your workflows, your company's strategy deck, your coworker's suspiciously well-written status updates.

And honestly? For a lot of people, it's kind of hell.

Not the fun kind of hell, like a demanding video game level that eventually becomes satisfying when you conquer it. More like the dystopian kind, where you can feel the machinery of something larger shifting around you and you're not entirely sure if you're the operator or the fuel. The whole thing feels like a precursor to robots just talking to each other — and in many cases, they already are. You're just the person who clicks "approve" between each exchange. Copy-paste, approve, copy-paste, approve. Your value-add is having a finger and an internet connection.

There's a special irony in the fact that the highest-value skills in this environment are things like: copy-pasting more effectively, writing slightly better prompts (instead of mashing the keyboard like I tend to do — a strategy that works more often than it should), and providing what's now being called "context." Remember "prompt engineering"? That pseudo-discipline that briefly tried to establish itself as a real career path before dying the kind of death all jargon deserves? Thank god that died before it could fully latch on. And now we've got "context engineering," which is essentially the same bullshit with a slightly fancier hat. These are fad terms that always beg the same question: _can't the AI just do this part too?_ And the answer is usually: yeah, probably, and it will soon. And then it does.

If you took a few years off from working right before all of this hit and came back in 2026, you'd need some serious catch-up time. Everything looks the same on the surface — same tools, same meetings, same company structures — but the way work actually gets _done_ underneath has fundamentally shifted. If nothing I've described in this chapter sounds familiar to you, then either this book isn't for you, or you are deeply, profoundly fucked. But it's okay — you can probably catch up. I'm not one of those people screaming "LEARN AI NOW OR DIE" on Twitter. That energy is exhausting, and also, they're usually trying to sell you a course.

The great normalization isn't a future event. It already happened. You're living in it. The question now isn't whether AI has changed work — it obviously has. The question is whether you're going to understand what that change actually means for your day-to-day life, or whether you're going to keep pretending that your "unique human perspective" is what's keeping you employed and not the fact that AI still needs someone to click the buttons.

Spoiler: it's the buttons.
